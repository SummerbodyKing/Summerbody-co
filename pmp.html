Exam-Day Patterns

The six patterns that answer most questions, built from every question you missed. Read every question for what already happened, then match it to one of these. Tap Read aloud for the walk.

1
Change control, first
Any change to scope, budget, schedule, or a baseline goes through a change request to the change control board. Even a great technical fix that costs money is a change. Re-estimating and validating are steps inside change control, not the first move.
2
Stakeholder or communication problem
Go to the stakeholder engagement plan and engage proactively. Do not replace people. Do not just monitor or document, that is reactive. Improve the communication.
3
Servant leadership
Remove the impediment and protect the team's focus. Do not pull a member off their work, do not turn a glitch into a new project, do not escalate first.
4
Agile scenario
Empower the self-organizing team, support the Product Owner, deliver value with an MVP or prototype, hold a sustainable pace. Never jam work into a running sprint, never use a predictive tool (SPI, fast track, CCB) in an agile question.
5
Value-driven decision
Continue if the value beats the remaining cost. Kill and re-charter if it is fundamentally broken (catastrophic CPI and SPI, major scope change, troubled history).
6
Prove value or get buy-in
Build a prototype or MVP. Something tangible beats documentation. It lets stakeholders see it and give early feedback.
✂️ Cross these out fast
  • Escalate to the sponsor or PMO first, almost always wrong.
  • Replace the people, wrong.
  • Just monitor or log it without acting, wrong, that is reactive.
  • Skip change control, wrong.
  • A predictive tool inside an agile scenario, wrong.
The mindset underneath it all: gather information first, never escalate or punish or reject first, serve the team, talk to people directly, and choose the most value-driven answer. Earned Value, The S-Curve

Three lines tell the whole story. PV is the plan, EV is what you earned, AC is what you paid. Where they sit tells you over or under, ahead or behind.
CV = EV − AC
Negative = over budget
SV = EV − PV
Negative = behind schedule
CPI = EV ÷ AC
Below 1 = over budget
SPI = EV ÷ PV
Below 1 = behind schedule
Memory anchor: Always start the formula with EV. Earned Value is the hero. Minus the other letter gives a variance, divided by the other letter gives an index. Below 1.0 or negative is always bad.

Tuckman, Team Development Stages

Every team climbs these five steps. When a question describes confusion or conflict, find the matching rung.
1
Forming
Team meets. Polite, unsure, dependent on the PM for direction.
2
Storming
Conflict over roles and approach. Friction, little output. (Most "team is arguing" questions live here.)
3
Norming
Team agrees on ground rules, builds trust, settles into a rhythm.
4
Performing
High output, self-organizing, minimal PM intervention needed.
5
Adjourning
Work is done, team disbands, lessons learned captured.
Trigger words: "confusion over roles" and "arguing about approach" = Storming. "Highly productive" = Performing. The PM's coaching style changes per stage: directing, then coaching, then supporting, then delegating.
🤝

Conflict Resolution Modes

Thomas-Kilmann grid: how assertive vs how cooperative. PMI's favorite is always Collaborate.
▲ More assertiveMore cooperative ▶

Forcing / Compete

Impose your decision. Win-lose. Fast but damages relationships.

Collaborate ⭐

Open up the disagreement, find a win-win. PMI's preferred mode, every time it is an option.

Avoid / Withdraw

Sidestep the conflict. Lose-lose. Only buys time.

Smooth / Accommodate

Emphasize agreement, downplay the difference. Temporary peace.

Compromise (the middle)

Both sides give something up. Lose-lose-ish. Good when time is short and stakes are even, but never the best answer when Collaborate is offered.

Exam default: When two people disagree and you can pick "facilitate a discussion to find a solution both accept," that is Collaborate. Choose it unless the question blocks it.

Stakeholder Power / Interest Grid

How much can they affect the project (power) vs how much do they care (interest). That decides how you engage them.
▲ High powerHigh interest ▶

Keep Satisfied

High power, low interest. Give them what they need, do not overload. (Senior execs not close to the work.)

Manage Closely ⭐

High power, high interest. Frequent, detailed updates. They can make or break you. (The sponsor, the CEO who cares.)

Monitor

Low power, low interest. Minimal effort, occasional check-in.

Keep Informed

Low power, high interest. Keep them in the loop. (End users, store managers.)

Remember: High power + high interest = Manage Closely. That CEO with high interest is not "keep satisfied," they are "manage closely."

Risk Response Strategies

Threats and opportunities are mirror images. Memorize both columns side by side.
Threats (negative)
A
Avoid
Eliminate the threat. Change the plan so it cannot happen.
T
Transfer
Shift impact to a third party. Insurance, warranty, outsourcing.
M
Mitigate
Reduce probability or impact. Add testing, build redundancy.
A
Accept
Take no action, set a contingency. Active or passive.
Opportunities (positive)
E
Exploit
Make sure it happens. The opposite of avoid.
S
Share
Partner with a third party who can best capture it.
E
Enhance
Increase probability or impact. The opposite of mitigate.
A
Accept
Take it if it comes, do not chase it.
Escalate is the fifth option for both: when the risk is outside your authority or project scope, hand it to the right level. Insurance is always Transfer.

Predictive ↔ Hybrid ↔ Agile

It is a spectrum, not a switch. Half the exam lives on the right side. Keywords tell you where you are.
🏗️🔀🏃
Predictive / Waterfall
Fixed scope, plan upfrontHybrid
Best of bothAgile / Scrum
Adapt, iterate, deliver value fast

Predictive keywords

WBS, Gantt, baseline, Change Control Board (CCB), change request, phase gate, scope statement, critical path.

Agile keywords

Sprint, backlog, velocity, Product Owner, Scrum Master, retrospective, Definition of Done, burndown, iteration, story points.

First move on every situational question: spot the keywords and lock the environment. In predictive you submit a change request. In agile you add it to the backlog. Same situation, different correct answer.

Cost of Quality

Pay a little now to prevent, or pay a lot later to fix. Prevention is always cheapest.

Prevention

Before defects: training, documented process, requirement reviews, test plans. (Cost of Conformance)

Appraisal

During: inspection, testing, audits to catch defects. (Cost of Conformance)

Internal Failure

Found before delivery: rework, scrap. (Cost of Non-Conformance)

External Failure

Found after delivery: warranty, recalls, lost trust. The most expensive of all.

QA vs QC: Quality Assurance = auditing the PROCESS (are we following standards? proactive). Quality Control = inspecting the DELIVERABLE (does the output meet spec? reactive).

Contract Types, Who Holds the Risk

Slide from fixed price to cost plus and the risk moves from seller to buyer.
🧱⚖️💸
Fixed Price (FP)
Seller holds risk.
Buyer safest. Scope must be clear.Time & Materials
Shared. Good for staff augmentation.Cost Plus (CPFF/CPIF)
Buyer holds risk.
Seller safest. Use when scope is fuzzy.
Pick by certainty: Well-defined scope → Fixed Price protects the buyer. Uncertain scope → Cost Plus protects the seller. Insurance against a specific risk is risk Transfer, not a contract type.

Motivation Theories at a Glance

The names PMI loves to test, boiled down to one line each.
M
Maslow's Hierarchy
Physiological → Safety → Social → Esteem → Self-actualization. Lower needs first.
H
Herzberg Two-Factor
Hygiene (salary, security) stops dissatisfaction but does not motivate. Motivators (recognition, growth, challenge) actually drive people.
XY
McGregor X & Y
Theory X: people avoid work, must be controlled. Theory Y: people are self-motivated. PMI leans Y.
V
Vroom Expectancy
Motivation = belief that effort leads to reward that I value.
M3
McClelland Needs
Achievement, Affiliation, Power. Match the task to what drives the person.
Classic question: someone already paid well wants more motivation. Herzberg says more salary is a hygiene factor, it will not motivate. Give recognition, challenge, and growth instead.

The PMI Mindset, 3-Step Method

Muhammad's framework. This is how you solve any situational question, even ones you have never seen.

1 · Understand

Strip the fluff. Restate the real problem in your own words before looking at answers.

2 · Eliminate

Kill answers that contradict the mindset. Never delay, never escalate first, never reject, never blame.

3 · Choose

Pick the answer that fully solves the problem. Gather-info-first is almost always right when offered.

PMI always does this

Gather info first · Be a servant leader · Remove blockers · Trust the team (they are the experts) · Collaborate · Refer to the plan and lessons learned · Deliver value · Close the project formally, always.

PMI never does this

Escalate first · Reject a request outright · Blame or punish · Remove a team member to "fix" things · Accept a delay · Gold-plate (add unapproved scope) · Use the words only / always / never.
