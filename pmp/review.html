The Twenty Mnemonics

Mnemonic 1

POC before CCR. Proof of Concept before Change Request.

Before submitting a formal change request, assess impact first. The change request always carries data with it.

Mnemonic 2

Register lists. Plan processes. Matrix measures.

Stakeholder Register is the list of who is involved. Stakeholder Engagement Plan is the process for managing expectations. Stakeholder Assessment Matrix is the tool for measuring current versus desired engagement.

Mnemonic 3

Audience changes equal Comms Plan updates.

When the who you are communicating with changes, update the Communications Management Plan.

Mnemonic 4

Complaints or declined meetings equal Comms Plan.

When stakeholders are unhappy or skipping meetings, the root cause is usually a missing or weak Communications Management Plan.

Mnemonic 5

Risk first. Motivate the marathon.

In agile, high risk stories go in early sprints, not saved for later. Motivation is ongoing through a benefits system, not a party at Go-Live.

Mnemonic 6

Can't control it? Adjust my plan.

When someone else causes a delay, you do not ask them to move faster. You adjust your own timeline. Do not bypass required work. You have missed this three times. Do not miss it tomorrow.

Mnemonic 7

PMO across. Sponsor one project. PM executes.

The PMO is enterprise wide and the integral stakeholder across all projects. The Sponsor champions and funds one specific project. The PM executes. The Customer uses the output.

Mnemonic 8

Comply and explain. One on one. Steering committee later.

When a stakeholder resists a compliance requirement, the first move is a direct one on one conversation with them to explain the need. Never exclude, never sneak, never jump to a steering committee first.

Mnemonic 9

Purpose before people. Purpose before analysis.

The first step on any new project is to define vision and purpose. Then identify stakeholders. Then resources. Then detailed analysis. Never skip ahead to schedule, impact analysis, or benchmarking.

Mnemonic 10

Tailor before build. Use what exists first.

When starting a new project in an organization with existing assets, adapt and tailor what exists first. Don't jump to procurement or building from scratch.

Mnemonic 11

Match behavior to orientation.

A Directive PM uses clear task focused communication. An Affiliation PM builds rapport. An Achievement PM uses awards. A Power PM uses influence. If the question tells you the PM's orientation, match your answer to that orientation.

Mnemonic 12

Unexpected loss equals Notify plus Investigate plus Risk Register.

When something goes wrong, an asset is lost, or damage occurs, notify both customer and management, launch an investigation, update the risk register, then take action based on the outcome.

Mnemonic 13

Mapping is prep. Workshops build.

Stakeholder mapping identifies who they are. Interactive workshops actually build relationships and shared understanding. If PMI asks about building, pick workshops, not mapping.

Mnemonic 14

Issues now. Risks future. Lessons after.

Issue log is for problems happening now. Risk register is for uncertainty about the future. Lessons learned is for future projects, after this one is done.

Mnemonic 15

Universal PM quality equals honesty. Servant leadership equals agile only.

When PMI asks what quality a PM must have, the universal answer is honesty. Servant leadership is agile specific, not universal.

Mnemonic 16

Manager controls processes. Leader inspires people.

Manager focuses on control, efficiency, authority, and objectives. Leader focuses on vision, inspiration, alignment, and motivation.

Mnemonic 17

No historical data? Build the risk plan yourself.

When historical data is unavailable, develop the risk management plan from scratch and inform stakeholders. Just telling the sponsor and continuing is not enough.

Mnemonic 18

Plan prevents. Audit detects. Update fixes.

When something goes wrong, the preventive root cause fix is a proper Risk Management Plan with monitoring built in. Not internal audits. Not template updates. Not blame.

Mnemonic 19

Insurance equals Transfer. Not just recording.

The four risk responses are Avoid, Transfer, Mitigate, and Accept. Insurance is a classic transfer strategy. Just recording a risk in the register is bookkeeping, not a strategy.

Mnemonic 20

Delphi identifies remotely. Monte Carlo and Decision Tree are quantitative analysis. Affinity is qualitative organizing.

Delphi technique uses anonymous polling. It is great for distributed and remote teams. It is a risk identification tool. Monte Carlo simulates cost and schedule. Decision Tree calculates probability. Affinity Diagram organizes ideas by category. Only Delphi is for identification. The others are for analysis.

Risk Deep Dive

Risk is your number one weak zone. Learn this section cold.

The Risk Process Order. Memorize as PIQQ.

First: Plan Risk Management. Create the methodology document.

Second: Identify Risks. List them out.

Third: Qualitative Risk Analysis. Prioritize by probability and impact. This is subjective and fast.

Fourth: Quantitative Risk Analysis. Assign numbers to the top priority risks. This is Monte Carlo and Expected Monetary Value. It is expensive.

Qualitative equals prioritize. Quantitative equals quantify.

After Qualitative Risk Analysis, Four Documents Update. Remember RRAI.

Risk Register. Individual risks with their new priority.

Risk Report. Overall project risk exposure.

Assumption Log. Assumptions validated or invalidated during analysis.

Issue Log. Risks that have become actual issues.

You keep picking only Risk Register and Risk Report. Remember. Four documents. R R A I.

External Volatility Equals Risk Register.

When the question mentions financial market changes, exchange rates, regulatory shifts, supplier volatility, or political and economic conditions — the document you refer to is always the Risk Register.

The Four Risk Response Strategies.

Avoid. Don't do the project or eliminate the risk source.

Transfer. Shift to a third party. Insurance, bonds, warranties, contracts. There is a premium cost.

Mitigate. Reduce probability or reduce impact.

Accept. Passive means do nothing. Active means contingency reserves.

Reserves.

Contingency Reserve is for known unknowns. It is in the cost baseline.

Management Reserve is for unknown unknowns. It is not in the cost baseline.

Motivation Theories

You keep confusing these. Learn the trigger word for each.

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs.

Trigger words: basic needs, safety, belonging, esteem, self actualization. Lower needs must be met first.

Expectancy Theory. From Vroom.

Trigger words: reward, compensation, if they perform they'll get X. Motivated when confident, valuing the reward, and trusting you will pay.

McClelland's Achievement Theory.

Trigger words: need for achievement, need for power, need for affiliation. People are motivated by one of three drives.

Herzberg's Two Factor.

Trigger words: hygiene factors, job satisfaction versus dissatisfaction. Salary avoids dissatisfaction. Growth motivates.

McGregor Theory X and Y.

Theory X assumes people are lazy. Theory Y assumes people are self motivated.

Theory Z from Ouchi.

Trigger words: loyalty, long term employment, Japanese style. Team based and consensus driven.

Fiedler's Contingency Theory.

Trigger words: leadership style depends on situation. No one right style. It depends on context.

Common trap. Reward for performance equals Expectancy, not Maslow. Basic needs equals Maslow, not Expectancy.

Hybrid Meta Rule

On hybrid questions, your brain reaches for generic good PM answers. PMI wants the specific answer to the specific question.

Read every hybrid question twice.

Ask yourself: is PMI asking for a universal answer, or a situational answer?

If the question tells you the PM's orientation, situation, or context — match your answer to that specific context.

If the question asks about a must have for all PMs — pick the universal answer. Honesty, transparency, ethics.

Do not pick servant leader for a universal question. Do not pick build rapport for a directive PM. Do not pick stakeholder mapping when the ask is to build relationships.

When You Don't Know. The Decision Tree.

Step One. Eliminate the two extreme answers.

Immediately halt is usually wrong. It is an overreaction.

Ignore it is usually wrong. It is irresponsible.

Never permitted is usually wrong. It is too rigid.

Step Two. Of the two remaining, pick the more PM like.

The one that follows process beats the one that skips it.

The one that involves the right stakeholder beats the one that goes over their head.

The one with fewer assumptions beats the one requiring you to invent context.

Step Three. Check for keyword traps.

First means the earliest legitimate step, not the most action heavy sounding.

Best means the one that solves the root cause, not the symptom.

Most appropriate means the one that follows PMI's ethical and process framework.

Step Four. Agile versus Predictive.

If the question mentions iteration, sprint, user story, product owner — it is Agile.

If it mentions baseline, phase, CCB, change request — it is Predictive.

Step Five. When two answers seem equally good.

Pick the one that involves the Sponsor or the Team. Not the one that goes to HR or Legal or documents it. PMI rewards human connection over paperwork.
